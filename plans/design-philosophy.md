# Timber Chronology

A design philosophy for Abuzar Industries.

## Manifesto

**Timber Chronology** treats wood not as material but as a record of time itself — annual rings as data points, grain as calligraphy, patina as evidence of endurance. This is a visual language built from the systematic observation of organic matter: the cross-section as a map, the knot as a marker, the split as a story. It borrows the rigor of scientific cataloguing and applies it to the living, breathing substance of timber.

**Space and form.** Compositions are constructed as field studies — dense accumulations of mark-making that reward prolonged looking. Forms emerge from repetition: stacked plank silhouettes, cross-section circular motifs, grain-line contours. The negative space between these forms reads as the air in a timber yard — vast, quiet, filled with the weight of stacked wood. Nothing is decorative; every mark is a data point in the chronology.

**Color and material.** The palette comes from the wood itself: heartwood brown, sapwood cream, aged-teak amber, neem's olive-grey, white teak's pale honey. Accents of gold leaf ochre reference the sunlight that grew the tree. The background is warm, fibrous, paper-like — evoking the surface of untreated wood. Blacks and near-blacks are used sparingly, like ink stamps on a forester's log.

**Scale and rhythm.** Large typographic gestures function as tree-trunk scale — bold, upright, anchoring the composition. Smaller text elements are spaced like timber stock records: methodical, ledger-like, referencing the systematic nature of the trade. Repetition of circular forms echoes the cross-section of logs — a motif that recurs like tree rings throughout.

**Composition and balance.** The work is anchored by a central mass — like the trunk of a great tree — with information radiating outward in layers: bark (outer details), cambium (context), heartwood (the core message). This radial hierarchy mimics the structure of wood itself. Balance is asymmetrical but weighted, like a well-stacked timber cart.

**Craftsmanship.** Every piece produced under this philosophy is the work of a master timber craftsman who has spent thirty years learning to read wood. The precision of the typography, the deliberate placement of every mark, the calibration of every color — these are not design decisions but acts of respect for the material. The final work must appear as though it took countless hours at a workbench, because understanding wood takes a lifetime.
